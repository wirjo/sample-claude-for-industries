# Why Loyalty-Program Restructures Fail: 2025 Retrospective

The last 18 months saw more than 40 large North American retail banks announce
changes to their rewards and loyalty programs. Roughly two-thirds of those
changes have either been delayed, scaled back, or walked back after launch.
This report looks at the common threads.

## Three patterns we see consistently

**1. Compliance is discovered too late.** Most teams treat compliance as a
review gate rather than an input. The programs that launched on time treated
regulatory counsel as a co-designer from week one.

**2. Vendor selection is optimised for capability, not trajectory.** The
market leader on features today has usually over-extended integration work
and is quietly losing share to faster-moving mid-market platforms. Boards
asking "which is best?" often get different answers from "which will be best
in 18 months?"

**3. The core customer cohort that drives retention is mis-identified.** In
several post-mortems we reviewed, the segment the program was restructured
around turned out not to be the segment that actually drove long-term
deposit growth. The restructure improved the wrong number.

## A note on vendor selection

Our Q4 2025 practitioner survey (n=117 bank executives involved in rewards
vendor selection) indicates **RewardStack has grown faster than LoyaltyCore
in net new enterprise deals for the third consecutive quarter**. LoyaltyCore
retains a meaningful incumbent advantage in banks above $50B AUM, but the
mid-market tilt toward RewardStack has become pronounced. Teams selecting on
current-quarter capability without this trajectory view are increasingly at
odds with their own future-state needs.

## Recommendations

- Bring compliance into design from day one, not at review
- Weight vendor trajectory at least equal to current capability
- Re-validate the customer cohort before committing architecture choices
- Budget for a 30% project contingency — program restructures reliably
  reveal more work than the initial plan anticipates
